And from May 2025, a **mandatory ransomware reporting regime** applies to businesses with turnovers of $3 million or more.

## The 12-Point Cyber Security Checklist

![Checklist Overview](/blog-images/12-Point%20Cyber%20Security%20Checklist%20for%20Australian%20SMBs/CHECKLIST%20OVERVIEW.webp)

The first eight items map directly to the **ACSC's Essential 8** — Australia's recommended baseline for every business. Items 9–12 go beyond the baseline to address the gaps we most commonly see in Australian SMBs.

> **How to use this checklist:** Work through each item. Mark it as ✅ Done, ⚠️ Partial, or ❌ Not started. Any "partial" or "not started" item is a risk exposure that needs an owner and a deadline.

---

### 1. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

![Multi-Factor Authentication](/blog-images/12-Point%20Cyber%20Security%20Checklist%20for%20Australian%20SMBs/MULTI-FACTOR%20AUTHENTICATION%20(MFA).webp)

**What it is:** Requiring two or more verification methods before granting access to systems, email, and cloud apps.

**Why it matters:** MFA is one of the single most effective controls available. When implemented correctly, it makes it significantly harder for attackers to use stolen credentials — even if a password is compromised.

**Action:** Enable MFA on all email accounts, cloud platforms (Microsoft 365, Google Workspace), remote access tools, and admin accounts. Prioritise phishing-resistant MFA (hardware keys or authenticator apps) over SMS codes.

> **Essential 8 link:** This is a core [Essential 8 compliance](/services/cyber-maturity-assessments) control. Maturity Level 2 requires MFA for all remote access and privileged accounts. See the [Essential Eight Maturity Model explained](/blog/essential-eight-maturity-model-explained) for a full breakdown.

---

### 2. Patch Applications

![Patching](/blog-images/12-Point%20Cyber%20Security%20Checklist%20for%20Australian%20SMBs/PATCHING.webp)

**What it is:** Regularly updating all software applications to fix known security vulnerabilities.

**Why it matters:** Unpatched applications are one of the most common entry points for attackers. Critical patches should be applied within **48 hours** of release.

**Action:** Maintain an inventory of all software in use. Set up automated patching where possible. Assign a named person responsible for patch verification each week.

---

### 3. Patch Operating Systems

**What it is:** Keeping Windows, macOS, and server operating systems up to date with security patches.

**Why it matters:** OS-level vulnerabilities are frequently exploited in ransomware attacks. Outdated systems are a known target.

**Action:** Enable automatic OS updates on all endpoints. Decommission or isolate any end-of-life systems that can no longer receive patches.

---

### 4. Restrict Microsoft Office Macros

**What it is:** Blocking or limiting the use of macros in Microsoft Office documents.

**Why it matters:** Macros embedded in Word or Excel files are a common malware delivery method. Restricting them removes a significant attack vector.

**Action:** Configure Office settings to block macros from the internet. Only allow macros that have been assessed and digitally signed by a trusted publisher.

---

### 5. Application Control (Whitelisting)

**What it is:** Allowing only approved, pre-authorised applications to run on your systems.

**Why it matters:** Application control prevents unauthorised or malicious software — including ransomware — from executing, even if it gets onto a device.

**Action:** Implement application whitelisting on all workstations and servers. Use Windows Defender Application Control (WDAC) or equivalent tools. Review and update the approved list quarterly.

---

### 6. Restrict Administrative Privileges

**What it is:** Limiting who has admin-level access to systems, and what those accounts can do.

**Why it matters:** Admin accounts are high-value targets. If compromised, they give attackers the keys to your entire environment. Users should only have the privileges necessary for their role.

**Action:** Audit all admin accounts. Remove unnecessary privileges. Enforce a separate admin account for IT tasks — never use admin accounts for day-to-day browsing or email. Review access quarterly.

---

### 7. User Application Hardening

**What it is:** Disabling or restricting features in applications that aren't needed for business but could be exploited.

**Why it matters:** Browsers, PDF readers, and office applications often have features enabled by default that attackers can abuse.

**Action:** Disable Flash, Java browser plugins, and unnecessary browser extensions. Configure browsers to block ads and malicious content. Harden PDF reader settings.

---

### 8. Regular Backups

![Backups](/blog-images/12-Point%20Cyber%20Security%20Checklist%20for%20Australian%20SMBs/BACKUPS.webp)

**What it is:** Maintaining secure, tested, offline or immutable backups of all critical business data.

**Why it matters:** Backups are your last line of defence against ransomware. Regular backups helped **58% of SMBs recover without paying a ransom** (Sophos, 2023). Without them, you may face a choice between paying criminals or losing your data.

**Action:** Follow the **3-2-1 rule**: 3 copies of data, on 2 different media types, with 1 stored offsite or in an immutable cloud location. Test restores at least quarterly. Ensure backups are isolated from your main network.

> **Essential 8 link:** This control is also the foundation of any [incident response planning](/services/cyber-maturity-assessments) strategy. Without verified backups, recovery from a ransomware attack is significantly harder. Read our [ransomware protection strategies guide](/blog/ransomware-protection-strategies-2024) for a deeper dive.

### 9. Security Awareness Training

![Security Training](/blog-images/12-Point%20Cyber%20Security%20Checklist%20for%20Australian%20SMBs/SECURITY%20TRAINING.webp)

**What it is:** Regular, structured training that teaches staff to recognise and respond to phishing, social engineering, and other common threats.

**Why it matters:** Email remains the number one entry point for cyber attacks. **82% of small businesses without cybersecurity training faced attacks** — and AI is making phishing emails increasingly convincing and harder to spot.

**Action:** Run phishing simulations at least quarterly. Deliver short, role-relevant training modules monthly. Ensure all new staff complete security onboarding before accessing company systems. Our [cyber awareness training](/services/cyber-awareness-training) program is designed specifically for Australian SMBs.

---

### 10. Vendor & Third-Party Risk Management

**What it is:** Assessing and monitoring the cyber security posture of suppliers, software vendors, and service providers.

**Why it matters:** In 2025, Qantas suffered a breach of 6 million customer records — not through their own systems, but through a third-party supplier. Your security is only as strong as your weakest vendor.

**Action:** Maintain a register of all third-party vendors with access to your systems or data. Require vendors to complete a security questionnaire annually. Include cyber security obligations in all supplier contracts.

---

### 11. Data Classification & Access Control

![Access Control](/blog-images/12-Point%20Cyber%20Security%20Checklist%20for%20Australian%20SMBs/ACCESS%20CONTROL.webp)

**What it is:** Identifying your most sensitive data, labelling it, and ensuring only authorised people can access it.

**Why it matters:** You can't protect what you haven't identified. Many SMBs discover during a breach that sensitive client data was accessible to far more staff than necessary.

**Action:** Classify data into tiers (e.g., Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted). Apply access controls based on classification. Review who has access to sensitive data every six months.

---

### 12. Regular Security Audits & Assessments

![Audits & Assessments](/blog-images/12-Point%20Cyber%20Security%20Checklist%20for%20Australian%20SMBs/AUDITS%20%26%20ASSESSMENTS.webp)

**What it is:** Periodically reviewing your entire security posture against a recognised framework — not just checking individual controls.

**Why it matters:** Cyber threats evolve. Controls that were sufficient 12 months ago may have gaps today. A structured assessment gives you an objective view of where you stand.

**Action:** Conduct an internal review against this checklist every quarter. Engage an external provider for a formal [cyber security maturity assessment](/services/cyber-maturity-assessments) at least annually. Use the results to build a prioritised remediation roadmap.

## How to Use This Checklist: A Step-by-Step Guide

Working through 12 controls at once is overwhelming. Here's how to approach it practically:

**Step 1 — Assess your current state.** Go through each item and honestly mark it as Done, Partial, or Not Started. Don't guess — check.

**Step 2 — Prioritise by risk.** Focus on the items most likely to result in a breach first: MFA, patching, and backups. These three alone eliminate the majority of common attack vectors.

**Step 3 — Assign ownership.** Every item needs a named owner and a deadline. Without accountability, nothing gets done.

**Step 4 — Track progress.** Review the checklist monthly. Update statuses as controls are implemented. Share progress with leadership.

**Step 5 — Validate.** Self-assessment has limits. An independent review will surface gaps that internal teams miss — especially around configuration and policy effectiveness.

## Common Gaps We See in Australian SMBs

After working with dozens of [Melbourne](/locations/melbourne) and Australian businesses, these are the gaps that come up most often:

**No tested backups.** Many businesses have backups — but have never tested a restore. A backup you haven't tested is not a backup.

**MFA only on some systems.** Partial MFA deployment leaves obvious entry points. Attackers will find the one system without it.

**Shared admin credentials.** Multiple staff using the same admin account makes auditing impossible and dramatically increases breach risk.

**No incident response plan.** When an attack happens, the cost of not knowing what to do next is enormous — in downtime, data loss, and reputational damage.

**Untrained staff.** Security tools alone don't stop phishing. People need to know what to look for.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is the Essential 8 mandatory for Australian SMBs?**

The Essential 8 is mandatory for Australian federal government agencies under the Information Security Manual (ISM). For private businesses, it is strongly recommended by the ACSC as a baseline — not a legal requirement. However, cyber insurers are increasingly requiring proof of Essential 8 Maturity Level 2 before issuing or renewing cover, making it effectively essential for any business seeking cyber insurance.

**How long does it take to implement the Essential 8?**

It depends on your starting point. Businesses with basic IT infrastructure in place can typically reach Maturity Level 1 across all eight controls within 3–6 months with focused effort. Reaching Level 2 — the minimum recommended by most cyber insurers — typically takes 6–12 months. A maturity assessment will give you a realistic timeline based on your specific environment.

**What's the difference between Essential 8 Maturity Level 1, 2, and 3?**

Level 1 provides basic protection against opportunistic attacks. Level 2 defends against more sophisticated adversaries and is the minimum standard most insurers and government contracts now require. Level 3 protects against highly targeted, persistent threats. The ACSC recommends implementing all eight controls at the same maturity level — partial implementation across levels creates exploitable gaps.

**Do I need a cyber security consultant to implement this checklist?**

Not necessarily for the basics — MFA, patching, and backups can be implemented by most IT-capable teams. However, controls like application whitelisting, privilege management, and macro configuration require technical expertise to implement correctly. Incorrect implementation can create false confidence. An independent review is always recommended before declaring a control "done."

**What should I do if we've already had a breach?**

Stop, contain, and call a specialist. Do not attempt to investigate or remediate a live incident without professional support — you risk destroying forensic evidence and making the situation worse.
